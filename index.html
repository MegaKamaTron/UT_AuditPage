Chapter Summary

"A woman who has optimized her life for the absence of surprise gets surprised."

Chapter One introduces Saki not through what she says about herself, but through what she fixes, avoids, and notices when she should be looking away. An email asking her to redo a week's work. A colleague she feeds coconut water to, more out of not wanting him to die at the desk beside her than out of warmth. A boss whose "family" speech she has committed to memory. A bus she misses by four seconds. And then a woman arriving at a bus stop completely soaked, with the bad timing of someone who was not supposed to matter. The chapter's structural argument is quiet: everything Saki does is designed to minimize contact with the world, and the one moment she abandons that design — tilting an umbrella toward a stranger — is the one the whole story will hinge on. The apartment at the end is the chapter's exhale. The fish. The mug. The crack in the ceiling she has never reported.

Why It Matters
01
The Mousepad
Before I leave, I notice my mouse pad is slightly crooked. I fix it out of instinct.

This line costs nothing and earns everything. Saki straightens the mousepad on her way out the door of a day that cost her hours she won't get back. The reflex is not about neatness — it is about the one thing she can still control in a space that asked too much of her. Keep this line exactly as written. Protect it from any edit that might try to give it commentary.

02
The Fish Tank
Evening. / One turns toward me. Blank eyes. Slow fins. / Must be nice. Doing nothing.

Saki greets her fish before she takes off her coat. She does not find this strange. The envy in "Must be nice. Doing nothing." is the most emotionally honest moment in the apartment scene — not because it is funny, but because it is true and she does not linger on it. One tap of the glass. Flakes in. Move on. This is how she processes the day: small rituals, no dwelling.

03
The Ceiling Crack
There's a crack in the ceiling I've never reported to the landlord. / I've gotten used to it.

Newly planted in this audit. Two lines, no thematic signal, no elaboration. It arrives and passes. Saki mentions it the way she mentions anything structural about her apartment — flatly. Its function is not felt in Chapter One. It will be felt much later, when Harumi's absence makes Saki notice the crack again and understand, without naming it, what 'gotten used to' actually cost her.

Why this edit

Saki makes concrete observations, not philosophical ones. 'Rough day for everyone, it seems' is a soft generalization — the kind of line a narrator uses to create sympathy. Saki would notice the timing, not the sentiment. The replacement is also more useful dramatically: it establishes that Saki is paying attention to Harumi before she consciously decides to.
